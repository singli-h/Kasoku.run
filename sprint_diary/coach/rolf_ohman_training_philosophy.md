# Rolf Ohman Training Philosophy

## Overview

**Rolf Ohman** is a Swedish sprint coach and biomechanics expert with over 40 years of experience in elite-level track and field. He is the inventor of the **1080 Sprint** and **1080 Quantum** robotic training systems. His notable work includes:

- Former Assistant Head Coach for the Chinese National Track and Field Team
- Helped Su Bingtian run **9.83** in the 100m (Asian record, Olympic final)
- Currently Head Coach at Hong Kong Sports Institute
- Collaborated extensively with **Randy Huntington** (world record holder in long jump coaching)
- Studied under **Charlie Francis** and adopted many of his principles
- Background in decathlon and Behavioral Science

---

## Core Philosophy Summary

> "Speed is the deciding factor - or rather, **acceleration** is."

Ohman's approach centers on:
1. **Velocity-based training** - Always move weights as fast as possible, regardless of load
2. **Eccentric capacity development** - The key to elastic power and injury prevention
3. **Time-specific training** - Match training velocities to sport demands (ground contact times)
4. **Optimal recovery** - Two-week training blocks with adequate CNS recovery
5. **Simplicity over complexity** - Fewer exercises done better, rather than many done poorly
6. **Progressive specificity** - Gradual progression from general to specific movements

## Fundamental Principles

### 1. The Physics of Speed

Two key equations govern Ohman's training philosophy:

```
Power = Force x Velocity
Force = Mass x Acceleration
```

**Key Insight**: If you cannot accelerate mass, you cannot produce force efficiently. Therefore:
- **Acceleration is king** - more important than raw power numbers
- Training must prioritize the ability to accelerate loads quickly
- Power means nothing if produced too slowly to transfer to the event

### 2. Time to Peak Velocity (TPV)

TPV is Ohman's most important metric - a measure of how quickly an athlete reaches peak velocity in a movement.

| Event/Movement | Typical TPV | Training Implication |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Max velocity sprinting | 90-100ms | Training must approach this speed |
| Long jump takeoff | 120-130ms | Exercises should match this |
| High jump takeoff | 180-240ms | Slightly longer acceptable |
| Traditional lifting | 250-350ms | **Too slow** for speed sports |

**Critical Principle**: If your training TPV is 180ms but your event requires 100ms, you have a **"gaposis"** problem - the gap between training and competition demands leads to:
- Poor transfer of training
- Increased injury risk
- No improvement despite getting "stronger"

### 3. The Dynamic Isometric Strength (DIS) Concept

When moving fast eccentrically (>80% of max capacity), something special happens:

1. The muscle enters a brief **isometric state** in the final degrees of motion
2. This stores massive amounts of **elastic energy**
3. The body must turn around within **~100ms** or energy dissipates as heat
4. This is an evolutionary protective mechanism for rapid movement

**Practical Application**: Fast eccentric training builds the capacity to:
- Store elastic energy rapidly
- Tolerate high eccentric loads
- Convert stored energy into explosive concentric power

## Periodization Model

### Block Structure: 2-Week Accumulation + 1-Week Recovery

Ohman rejects traditional 4-week training blocks for clean athletes:

```
Week 1-2: Accumulation (building phase)
Week 3: Recovery/Intensification (50% volume reduction)

Repeat for 2 cycles = 6 weeks = 1 block
2 blocks = 12 weeks = ~3 months for physiological adaptation
```

**Key Rules**:
- No clean athlete can sustain 4-week blocks at high intensity
- Recovery week maintains intensity but cuts volume by 50%
- CNS fatigue shows up first in TPV degradation
- Better to "undertrain" than risk injury

### Charlie Francis Influence: Speed Zones

| Zone | % Max | Purpose | CNS Impact |
|------|-------|---------|------------|
| Extensive Tempo | 70% | Recovery, aerobic capacity | Low |
| Training (never use) | 75-89% | **Dead zone** - avoid | Medium |
| Speed Endurance | 90-98% | Competition preparation | High |
| Max Velocity | 98-100% | Race simulation | Very High |

**Charlie Francis Rule**: "Do you race at 85%? Then why would you train at 85%?"
- Run extensive tempos at 70% OR run at 90%+
- Nothing in between - it's "no man's land"

### General Preparation Period (GPP)

For elite athletes, GPP should be:
- **2-3 weeks** for elite athletes (not 6-12 weeks!)
- Used to: grease joints, balance weaknesses, address injury sites
- Then immediately into specific work

As athletes develop, GPP shrinks:
- Youth: 6 weeks
- Junior: 4 weeks
- Senior: 3 weeks
- Elite: 2 weeks

## Strength Training Principles

### 1. Always Lift Fast

> "We lift fast all year round. It doesn't matter if we've got 190 kilos on the bar...we're trying to move it as fast as we can."

- Intent matters - even heavy loads should be moved with maximum effort
- 95% intent vs 100% intent can show 15-20% difference in metrics
- Every rep is a test of CNS readiness

### 2. Partial Range of Motion Training

**Controversial but essential**: Ohman uses partials extensively

**Why partials?**
- Shorter ROM = faster TPV = closer to sport demands
- Can maintain high loads while matching event speeds
- Reduces injury risk from excessive ROM under load

**Progression Example**:
```
Phase 1: Quarter box squats (TPV ~180ms)
Phase 2: 25cm step-ups (TPV ~120-130ms)
Phase 3: 10cm step-ups (TPV ~70ms)
```

At 10cm step-ups, athletes use **double body weight** but achieve TPVs matching sprint ground contact times.

### 3. Exercise Progression Scale (1-10)

Ohman rates exercises from 1 (general) to 10 (most specific):

```
1-3: GPP exercises, general movement patterns
4-6: Meat of training - where most adaptation occurs
7-9: Highly specific, used sparingly
10: Ferrari exercises - race simulation
```

**Rules**:
- Stay in a zone as long as you're improving
- Don't skip levels (1-2-3 then jump to 8-9 = injury)
- Progression happens year to year, not week to week

### 4. Contrast Training

Always pair heavy movements with fast/elastic movements:

```
Set 1: Heavy box squats (180ms TPV)
Immediately followed by: Ankle pops with 20kg air (very fast)

Set 2: Heavy box squats
Followed by: Ankle pops

...continue
```

**Purpose**:
- Recruits motor units with heavy load
- Realizes them with fast elastic movement
- Never venture too far from elasticity

### 5. Sets x Reps Philosophy: 8x3 vs 3x8

For power development, set/rep structure matters enormously:

```
8 sets x 3 reps = 24 total reps
3 sets x 8 reps = 24 total reps

Same volume, VASTLY different results
```

**Why 8x3 is superior for power**:
- Peak performance occurs on rep 2 or 3, not rep 7 or 8
- Each set allows full CNS recovery
- Maximum intent possible on every rep
- By rep 6-7-8, velocity degrades significantly

**Key insight**: Best lift is usually the second or third rep. Fourth rep starts dropping. By sets of 8, you're training fatigue, not power.

### 6. Load Selection by TPV

> "I've got no idea how strong my athletes are. We never max out."

Instead of percentage-based loading, select weights based on achieving target TPV:

| Event | Target TPV in Gym | If TPV exceeds... |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Sprinting | <180ms | Stop loading, reduce ROM |
| Long jump | <150ms | Shorten ROM or reduce load |
| High jump | <200ms | Acceptable for longer movements |

**Load progression rule**: Increase weight ONLY if you can maintain or improve TPV. If TPV degrades, either:
1. Reduce weight
2. Shorten range of motion
3. Both

## The Elastic Acceleration Index (EAI)

A key metric Ohman developed:

```
EAI = Peak Velocity / Time to Peak Velocity
```

**What it measures**: Athlete's ability to load eccentrically and express it concentrically

**Development potential**:
- Untrained: EAI of 3-5
- After 3-4 months of training: EAI of 20+
- Elite sprinters/jumpers: Very high EAI

**Training implication**: Track EAI over time - if it plateaus or drops:
- Check recovery status
- Reassess loading parameters
- May indicate CNS fatigue

## Eccentric Training

### The 1080 System Philosophy

The 1080 Quantum/Sprint systems were designed to:
1. Load the eccentric phase with **velocity** (not just weight)
2. Turn around within 100ms (the elastic energy window)
3. Use **less resistance** in concentric phase
4. Provide data on both phases

### Eccentric Velocity Threshold

Slow eccentrics (3-5 seconds) = lengthening fibers, building capacity

Fast eccentrics (>80% max capacity) = triggers DIS response:
- Isometric catch phase
- Massive elastic energy storage
- Protective mechanism engaged

### Kaiser Equipment Protocol

Using air/mass combinations to progressively develop eccentric capacity:

```
Phase 1 (Accumulation): 100% mass (normal bar)
Phase 2 (Intensification): 75% mass / 25% air
Phase 3 (Accumulation): 75% mass / 25% air
Phase 4 (Intensification): 50% mass / 50% air
...continue progression
```

**Why air matters**: Air resistance "follows" you down - creates massive eccentric loading as you try to accelerate through the movement.

### Speed Endurance Progression (Ohman's Core Concept)

**Note**: This is Ohman's BASE progression for a SINGLE peak season (Australian model: December start → April championships). For Northern Hemisphere double-peak seasons, see the adapted model below.

**This section covers**: The fundamental intensity, volume, and recovery progressions that apply to ANY periodization model.

**Intensity Progression** (from Ohman transcript):
```
Month 1 (prep start): 90%
Month 2: 92%
Month 3: 94%
Month 4 (pre-competition): 96-98%
```

**Volume Progression** (decreases as intensity increases):
```
Early prep: 180m + 150m + 120m + 100m (high volume, lower intensity)
Mid prep:   150m + 120m + 90m
Late prep:  150m + 80m (lower volume, higher intensity)
```

**Recovery Time Progression** (critical detail from transcript):
- Recovery times **start longer** and progressively **reduce to 4 minutes**
- "Once we get to about 4 minutes of recovery, that's when we stop" - Ohman
- The 4-minute recovery is the **endpoint/benchmark**, not the starting point

**Scientific Guidelines for Recovery** (from research):
| Intensity | Recovery per 10m of running |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| 90-95% | 30-45 seconds per 10m |
| 95%+ | 60-90 seconds per 10m |

*Example: 150m at 95% = 150/10 × 60-90sec = 9-13.5 min recovery*
*At 4 min recovery for 150m, athlete must have built significant capacity*

**Competition Readiness Benchmark** (Ohman's test):
- Run 150m at pace equivalent to 19.7 for 200m
- Take only **4 minutes recovery**
- Then run 80m at pace equivalent to 10.0 flat for 100m
- When you can do this, you're ready for multiple rounds at championships

**Sources**:
- Carlo Vittori's original speed endurance concept: 60-80m reps, 2 min between sprints, 8 min between series, 90%→95% progression ([PMC6872694](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6872694/))
- Charlie Francis methodology: 1-2 min recovery per second of maximal sprinting ([SimpliFaster](https://simplifaster.com/articles/real-life-charlie-francis-speed-threshold/))

### Northern Hemisphere Double-Periodization Model

> ⚠️ **Source Note**: This section adapts Ohman's core principles (above) for Northern Hemisphere double-peak seasons. The periodization framework is derived from Charlie Francis methodology and scientific literature, not directly from Ohman's transcript.

**For athletes needing two peaks**: Small peak mid-May → Big peak late August

**How this relates to Ohman's progression above**:
- Uses the SAME intensity progression (90% → 92% → 94% → 96-98%)
- Uses the SAME volume reduction principle (high → moderate → low → race-specific)
- Uses the SAME recovery benchmark (progress toward 4-minute recovery)
- Applied TWICE with a transition period between peaks

#### Annual Overview

```
October-November:    GPP (General Preparation)
December-January:    SPP1 (Speed Endurance Build #1)
February-March:      Pre-Competition Phase 1
April-mid May:       Competition Phase 1 → SMALL PEAK
mid May-early June:  Transition/Recovery (1-2 weeks)
June-July:           SPP2 (Speed Endurance Build #2)
August:              Competition Phase 2 → BIG PEAK
September:           Off-season/Active Recovery
```

#### Block 1: Building to Mid-May Peak

| Month | Focus | Intensity | Volume | Recovery Target |
|-------|-------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| January | Speed Endurance Base | 90% | High (180+150+120+100m) | 8-10 min |
| February | Intensification | 92% | Moderate (150+120+90m) | 6-8 min |
| March | Pre-Competition | 94% | Lower (150+80m) | 5-6 min |
| April | Competition Prep | 96% | Minimal (120+80m) | 4-5 min |
| May (early) | Peak | 98% | Race-specific | 4 min benchmark |

**Key principle**: This is your "smaller" peak - reach ~95% of your potential, not 100%.

#### Transition Period (Mid-May to Early June)

**Duration**: 7-14 days depending on competition load

**Week 1 post-competition**:
- Active recovery only
- Pool work, easy tempo (60-65%)
- No high-intensity CNS work
- Allow neural system to recover

**Week 2**:
- Gradual reintroduction of quality work
- Short accelerations (20-30m at 85%)
- Assess readiness via TPV metrics

#### Block 2: Building to Late August Peak

| Month | Focus | Intensity | Volume | Recovery Target |
|-------|-------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| Early June | Speed Endurance Rebuild | 88-90% | Moderate-High | 8-10 min |
| Late June | Build | 92% | Moderate | 6-8 min |
| July | Intensification | 94-96% | Lower | 5-6 min |
| Early Aug | Pre-Competition | 96-98% | Minimal | 4-5 min |
| Late Aug | Peak | 100% | Race-specific | 4 min benchmark |

**Key difference from Block 1**:
- You're building on existing fitness, not starting from scratch
- SPP2 is typically 6-8 weeks vs 10-12 weeks in SPP1
- Can reach higher intensity faster because base is established
- This is your MAIN peak - push to 100% of potential

#### Critical Considerations

**1. Don't "over-peak" in May**:
- Treat mid-May as a checkpoint, not the main event
- Reach 95% of potential, save the final 5% for August
- Avoid running maximum intensity races back-to-back

**2. Transition period is non-negotiable**:
- The CNS needs time to recover after competition phase
- Skipping this leads to "flatness" by August
- Better to lose 2 weeks than arrive fatigued at championships

**3. Block 2 is shorter but more intense**:
- Fitness base carries over (don't restart at 90%)
- Can begin SPP2 at higher intensity (88-90% vs 85%)
- Total time to peak: 10-12 weeks vs 16-18 weeks in Block 1

**4. Recovery time progression applies to BOTH blocks**:
- Always start with longer recovery (8-10 min)
- Progress toward 4-minute benchmark
- The 4-minute test validates readiness for championship rounds

#### Sample Week Structures by Phase

**Phase 1: SPP (Building) - e.g., June-July**
```
Monday:     High CNS #1 - Accelerations (3x30m, 2x40m @ 95%)
Tuesday:    Pool recovery + mobility
Wednesday:  Extensive tempo (70%)
Thursday:   High CNS #2 - Speed Endurance (150m + 120m @ 94%, 6-min recovery)
Friday:     Light technical work / drills
Saturday:   Extensive tempo or rest
Sunday:     OFF
```
**High-CNS days**: 2 (Monday + Thursday) - Ohman's standard recommendation

**Phase 2: Late Pre-Competition (8-12 weeks out) - e.g., early August**
```
Monday:     High CNS #1 - Accelerations + Weights
Tuesday:    Pool recovery / Mobility
Wednesday:  Extensive tempo (70%)
Thursday:   High CNS #2 - Max Velocity work
Friday:     Light technical / Tempo
Saturday:   High CNS #3 - Speed Endurance (90-96%)
Sunday:     OFF
```
**High-CNS days**: 3 - This is a TEMPORARY increase (4-8 weeks) as intensity builds toward peak. Volume per session is significantly reduced to compensate.

> **Note**: This temporarily exceeds Ohman's "max 2" guideline. It works because: (1) it's short-term, (2) volume is much lower, (3) the athlete has built capacity through prior blocks. Monitor TPV—if it degrades, drop back to 2 sessions.

**Phase 3: Competition (Racing) - see next section**

### Competition Phase: How Sessions Balance (Critical)

> **Phase Transition**: When racing begins, the weekly structure shifts. This section explains HOW sessions change from pre-competition (Phase 2 above) to competition phase.

**The Key Insight**: Racing REPLACES speed endurance sessions—you don't add races on top of existing training.

From Ohman transcript:
> "By the time we start competing, we are doing accelerations and we're doing max velocity, and then we bleed in the competition because **that becomes then speed endurance**."

#### Competition Phase Structure (Racing)

| Day | Session | Intensity |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| Monday | Accelerations (reduced volume) | 95%+ |
| Tuesday | Pool recovery / Mobility | Recovery |
| Wednesday | OFF or light tempo | <70% |
| Thursday/Friday | Max Velocity (reduced volume) | 95%+ |
| **Saturday** | **RACE** (replaces speed endurance) | **100%** |
| Sunday | OFF | - |

**High-CNS days**: 2 training sessions + 1 race = ~3 total CNS demands, but **volume dramatically reduced**
**Critical change**: Race IS your speed endurance—don't do both!

> **Reconciling "Max 2 high-CNS days" with 3 total**: Ohman's "max 2" rule refers to TRAINING sessions. During competition phase, you have 2 training sessions (reduced volume) + race. The race replaces a training session—total CNS load stays manageable because volume drops 50-80%.

#### The Balance Explained

**Why this works:**
1. **Racing at 100% > Training at 96-98%**: The race provides the highest-quality speed endurance stimulus
2. **Total CNS load stays constant**: 2 training sessions + 1 race ≈ previous 3 training sessions
3. **Recovery between high-CNS work**: 72+ hours maintained (Mon → Thu/Fri → Sat race)

**What changes approaching competition:**
- ✅ **Maintain intensity** (never drop below 95%)
- ✅ **Maintain frequency** (80%+ of training days)
- ❌ **Reduce volume** (50-80% reduction in final 2-4 weeks)
- ❌ **Eliminate accessories** (cut supplemental exercises)
- ❌ **Increase rest days** (1 every 6 → 1 every 2 → every other day near championships)

#### Taper Guidelines (Final 2-4 Weeks Before Major Peak)

| Weeks Out | Volume | Intensity | Rest Days |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| 4 weeks | 75% | Maintain | 1 per 5-6 days |
| 3 weeks | 60% | Maintain | 1 per 4 days |
| 2 weeks | 40-50% | Maintain | 1 per 2-3 days |
| Final week | 20-30% | Maintain | Every other day |

**Championship week example** ([2011 Worlds](https://www.freelapusa.com/tapering-for-speed-power-events-a-look-at-the-science/)):
- Eliminated accessory exercises completely
- Rest days increased from 1 every 6 days → every other day
- Intensity kept high throughout
- Result: Fresh CNS for multiple rounds

#### Ohman's Deload Philosophy

From transcript:
> "We deload by simply deloading the volume... intensity stays virtually the same... I'll cut the volume by 50 percent."

**Progressive deload as season advances:**
- Early season deloads: 25% volume reduction
- Mid-season deloads: 30-35% volume reduction
- Pre-championship: 50%+ volume reduction

#### What NOT To Do

❌ **Add races on top of full training** → CNS overload, flatness
❌ **Drop intensity during taper** → Lose neural adaptations
❌ **Skip deload weeks** → Accumulated fatigue compounds
❌ **Do speed endurance + race in same week** → Redundant CNS stress
❌ **Three high-CNS sessions + race** → Recipe for injury or flatness

## Recovery and Injury Prevention

### Why Ohman Has Never Had an Athlete Tear a Hamstring

1. **Adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions** (72-96 hours)
2. **Training velocities match competition demands**
3. **Progressive eccentric loading** builds tissue resilience
4. **Volume management** - better to undertrain than overtrain
5. **Listening to metrics** - TPV degradation = stop

### Recovery Between Sessions

- High intensity CNS work needs 72-96 hours
- "Availability is the best ability"
- Use technology to monitor readiness
- Easy test: 20kg bar squat jump - if TPV drops, CNS is fatigued

### Maximum High-Intensity Days Per Week

**Critical limit**: Maximum TWO high neural intensity days per week for most athletes.

Ohman tested three high-intensity days:
- Works for 2-3 weeks
- Then athletes are "finished"
- Risk of injury skyrockets

**Recovery sensitivity paradox**: As athletes get FASTER, they become MORE sensitive to neural fatigue (less tolerance, not more). Elite athletes need MORE recovery, not less.

### Testosterone Timing for Programming

Testosterone peaks **48 hours after velocity work**. This can inform session placement:
- Speed work Monday → Testosterone peaks Wednesday
- This timing can be used to optimize weekly structure

### The Injury Pattern

When training TPV is 250-350ms but competition requires 100-120ms:
- The system is unprepared for the speed
- Injury occurs at the gap between training and competition
- This was Ohman's personal experience (8 major operations as athlete)

## Counter-Movement Jump (CMJ) is NOT a Valid Speed Test

**Ohman's controversial stance**:

CMJ performed at 280-350ms TPV, but:
- Sprinters contact at 90-100ms
- Long jumpers at 120-130ms

Athletes can have:
- High CMJ (55cm+) but slow acceleration curve
- Low CMJ (45cm) but extremely fast acceleration curve

The second athlete has more speed potential but would be overlooked in traditional testing.

**Better approach**:
- Measure squat jump vs CMJ difference (should be 10cm+)
- Athletes who jump LESS in CMJ than squat jump have no elasticity
- This was common in hockey players before Ohman's intervention

## The Soleus: The Forgotten Muscle

Randy Huntington emphasized to Ohman: **"Your guys are too weak in the soleus."**

### Why Soleus Matters

- Stabilizes the entire lower leg at ground contact
- Absorbs and distributes impact forces
- Weak soleus = "wet rag hitting the track"
- Strong quads/glutes mean nothing if ankle complex can't stabilize

### Training Implications

- Seated calf raises (soleus isolation)
- Progressively faster calf work
- Don't neglect in favor of "big" muscle groups

## Critique of Soviet/Russian Training Methods

### Why High-Volume Soviet Methods Don't Work for Speed

> "The Russians are very good at weightlifting, hammer throwing, discus, shot put - events at 200-250ms. But they've never been able to produce world-class sprinters."

**The Soviet legacy problem**:
- Methods were built on drug use enabling superhuman recovery
- Clean athletes cannot sustain the volumes
- 2-2.5 meter drop jumps are useless and dangerous
- Nobody on earth can do a drop jump from 1 meter and still be fast enough

**What happens when Soviets met speed events**:
- Never produced 9.80 sprinters
- Pharmacology doesn't solve the neural speed problem
- You still need science and specific training to run fast

### The Chinese Overtraining Pattern

Asian athletes often have fantastic natural elasticity, but:
- Training systems destroy it with excessive volume
- Following outdated Soviet models
- Massive amounts of plyometrics that beat up the elastic system
- Mid-season "correction camps" that destroy fitness peaks

## Technical and Biomechanical Principles

### Usain Bolt vs. Asafa Powell: Technique Analysis

**Ohman's controversial take on Bolt**:
> "Bolt is pretty ordinary technically. He lives on his elasticity."

**Why Bolt succeeds despite technique**:
- His height (6'5") gives him extremely long levers
- Foot travels a longer arc = higher strike velocity into ground
- "Whipping" motion compensates for technical limitations
- Looks like pulling but is actually pushing down

**Technical model: Asafa Powell**:
- "Poetry in motion" - better technical model
- Demonstrates what pure technique looks like
- Better to study for coaching purposes

### Push vs. Pull Debate

Ohman's practical view:
- **Start to 25-30m**: Pushing (horizontal force application)
- **Max velocity**: Completely vertical push
- Su Bingtian: All straight-down striking (short legs = high cadence)
- Long jumpers may look like they're pulling but still pushing

### Height and Sprint Mechanics

| Height | Advantage | Challenge |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Tall (Bolt) | Long lever = high foot velocity | Harder to maintain cadence last 30m |
| Short (Su) | Extreme cadence, quick ground contact | Must "open stride" at max velocity |

## Comparing Grinding vs. Speed Training

### Characteristics of "Grinding" (Avoid)

- TPV > 250ms
- Focus on load over speed
- Traditional bodybuilding tempos
- "Get big and strong first, then get fast"
- 4+ week blocks without deload
- Volume over intensity

### Characteristics of Speed Training (Ohman Method)

- TPV < 180ms for speed athletes
- Every rep moved with max intent
- Fast eccentrics (>80% capacity)
- 2-week blocks with recovery
- Quality over quantity
- Progressive specificity

## Application for Sprint Training Planning

### When Reviewing a Sprint Training Plan, Check:

1. **Are high-intensity sessions spaced 72-96 hours apart?**
2. **Is there a dead zone (75-89%) being trained? Eliminate it.**
3. **Are weight room exercises producing appropriate TPV for the athlete's event?**
4. **Is there eccentric velocity work, not just slow eccentrics?**
5. **Are training blocks 2-3 weeks, not 4+ weeks?**
6. **Is there contrast work pairing heavy with fast?**
7. **Does GPP duration match athlete development level?**
8. **Is there progression from general to specific exercises?**
9. **Is volume managed appropriately (50% reduction in recovery weeks)?**
10. **Are ground contact times in plyometrics appropriate (<180ms)?**

### Plyometrics Guidelines

**Never do box jumps over 40cm** for speed athletes:
- Ground contact times become too slow
- No longer training the elastic system
- Athletes land "soft" to absorb impact = wrong adaptation

**Hurdle bound progressions**:
- Start with lower heights (wickets, mini-hurdles)
- Ground contact must be <180ms
- Quality over height/distance

### Red Flags in Sprint Programs

- Long runs at 75-89% intensity
- Ass-to-grass squats for speed athletes
- 4+ week blocks without deload
- No velocity tracking in weight room
- High hurdle/box jumps (>40cm contact times too slow)
- No eccentric velocity work
- Volume over intensity emphasis
- TPV not considered in exercise selection
- Three or more high-CNS days per week
- Mid-season "strength camps" (destroys fitness peak)
- Flywheel training for sprinters (not effective for this population)
- Nordic curls as primary hamstring work (prefer sliders + isometrics + slow eccentrics)

### Green Flags in Sprint Programs

- Clear speed zones (70% OR 90%+)
- Partial ROM exercises matching event demands
- Contrast training (heavy + fast paired)
- Progressive eccentric loading
- 2-week training blocks
- Metrics-driven decisions
- Adequate recovery emphasis
- Exercise progression system

## Cross-Pollination: Learning From Other Sports

### The Importance of Looking Outside Your Sport

> "Don't just look at your own sport. There are other sports doing very interesting things."

**Alpine Skiing Example**:
- Athletes face massive eccentric demands (3-4 second pushes at 130km/h)
- AND rapid landing forces (200ms with 5x body weight after jumps)
- Both extremes in the same 2-minute race
- Learning: Build "over-capacity" in speed/elasticity to operate at lower percentage during competition

**Ice Hockey Application**:
- No top-end speed needed (small rink)
- All about 3-4 diagonal skating pushes
- Acceleration and change of direction dominant
- Swedish team dominated after implementing Ohman methods

### Assisted Sprinting Insights

**Christian Taylor (Triple Jumper) Example**:
- Towed at **12.5 m/s** (faster than Usain Bolt's top speed)
- Demonstrates the "elastic threshold" concept
- Athletes can handle higher speeds than they can produce
- Builds neural pathways for faster limb speeds

## Nutrition Philosophy (Brief Overview)

For athletes, Ohman recommends approximately:
- **50% carbohydrates** (athletes NEED carbs)
- **30% protein**
- **20% fat**

**Common mistakes**:
- Female athletes under-eating ("skinny syndrome")
- Following keto/low-carb trends inappropriate for explosive sports
- Not understanding base metabolic needs (often 2500-2700+ calories for athletes)

> "80% of bodybuilding is nutrition. The training is only 20%."

## Summary: The Ohman Philosophy in One Page

1. **Speed is built on acceleration, not strength**
2. **TPV is the master metric** - if it's too slow, you're grinding
3. **Match training velocities to competition demands** (the gaposis principle)
4. **Two-week blocks maximum** for clean athletes
5. **70% OR 90%+** - nothing in between
6. **Always pair heavy with fast** (contrast training)
7. **Progress ROM from full to partial** as specificity increases
8. **Maximum two high-CNS days per week**
9. **Never box jumps over 40cm** for sprinters
10. **Build eccentric capacity progressively** - the key to injury prevention
11. **Technology for decision-making** - but only track 3-4 key metrics
12. **Simplify** - fewer exercises done better
