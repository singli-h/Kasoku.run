High speed running



Successful high-velocity training requires a balance between general training, likened to a savings account, and specific training, or the current account. General training includes exercises to improve overall athleticism while specific training focuses on sprinting performance. 



Quality of movement affects the rate of 'spending' in the current account - poor movement equates to higher 'costs'. If general training can't be maintained, care should be taken when introducing high-velocity training due to the potential for rapid resource depletion.



So on High speed running, it's all about quality instead of quantity. Treat it like a Max Weight Squat: 



- High volume will drain you out. 

- Once quality drops (running time increased 5-10%, form worsen), you should stop training. 

- There’s no point practicing running sub-max speed with worsen form caused by fatigue for the purpose of running at faster speed.



Neuromuscular adaptation:

High-intensity sprinting stimulates adaptations in the nervous system that improve the recruitment, synchronization, and firing rate of motor units (a motor neuron and the muscle fibers it innervates). This can lead to increases in power output and speed.

Slower running (Endurance, Tempo?)

Speed endurance training is about maintaining a high speed for longer periods. This requires the body to deal with fatigue factors such as lactic acid build-up.



Determining Pacing for Speed Endurance Runs

Calculate Target Pacing:

Typically, speed endurance runs are performed at around 90% of the athlete's maximum 100m sprint speed. This range ensures the athlete is working at a high intensity but still below their absolute max, allowing for the development of endurance at high speeds without excessive fatigue.

Use the Athlete's 100m PB to Calculate Pacing:

If the athlete's PB for 100m is 11.5s, calculate the speed first:

Speed = Distance / Time = 100m / 11.5s ≈ 8.70 m/s

Then, find 90% of this speed:

90% of 8.70 m/s ≈ 7.83 m/s

Apply Pacing to Different Distances:

If you're planning a speed endurance run over a different distance, like 150m, you can use the calculated pacing to find the required time:

150m at 7.83 m/s ≈ 19.15s

So at training we are looking for around the range 18.9-19.4s (under 0.5s difference for consistency)



Anaerobic Glycolysis

After the ATP-CP system is depleted (after about 10 seconds of maximum effort), the body relies on anaerobic glycolysis.



This process breaks down glucose for energy, producing lactate as a byproduct. Speed endurance training aims to improve the efficiency of this system, allowing the athlete to maintain a high speed for longer before fatigue sets in.



Lactate tolerance

High-intensity efforts cause an accumulation of lactate and H+ ions, which can interfere with muscle contractions and cause fatigue.



Training at high intensity can improve the body's ability to buffer these ions and clear lactate, thus improving speed endurance.

Periodization/Progression

Implementing a long-term training strategy that involves progressively increasing the intensity and volume of training to maximize performance and prevent overtraining.



Every periodisation model flows from general preparation to specific preparation to competition. General preparation involves slower speeds and simpler movements, while specific preparation introduces race-specific speeds and more complex techniques. The competition phase leverages adrenaline for full event execution.



Climate and competition calendars dictate different periodisation approaches, which can follow either a single or multiple sequence model.



The American collegiate program could be used as a single sequence moving from General in the fall, to specific indoors in winter and Competition outdoors from spring to early summer



The Jamaican model can be used as a single model moving from a larger/voluminous general phase in autumn and winter to a specific phase with some early competition and sometimes irregular distances in spring and moving to more regular competition distances as the summer season approaches



The European model, largely dictated by climate with inability to run outdoor events in March and April, can develop into a multiple periodisation model with general preparation followed by specific preparation followed by indoor competition with a repeated smaller process leading to a later start to outdoor competition in May



Single models allow for more time in each phase and a sequential pathway from slow to fast, while multiple models demand quicker movement through smaller phases and provide multiple opportunities for exposure to high-velocity inputs. Choose a periodisation approach that aligns with your local competition opportunities and allows for adequate preparation time.
